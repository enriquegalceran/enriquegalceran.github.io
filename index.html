UCM Observatory

Over the past years, I had the opportunity to teach a few courses on Observational Astronomy. This included teaching how to use and operate a telescope!

M31 Andromeda

This is one of the first objects I spent multiple days observing. I took this picture in a heavily light-poluted area, but the end result is still amazing!

M42 Orion Nebula

Sometimes you have to suffer for a great picture. In this case, I spent a full night in -10ºC snow and ice at the top of the mountain (severely underdressed... I learnt my lesson that night). The end result, however, is entirely worth it!

C/2025 R2 SWAN

Some images are easier than others. The first comet I've ever seen was C/2025 R2 SWAN, where I drove out to the middle of nowwhere with the Seestar S30. It's truly incredible what such a small and cheap telescope is capable of.

M45 Pleiades

An amazing congregation of stars, visible with the naked eye! I took this image from a light polluted area without a narrow-band filter, but it's so bright that it is still possible to take pictures of it despite the pollution!

Moon

Last summer I got a new reflector telescope. This allows for a far longer focal distance and greatly increase the augmentations. Together with a small planetary camera, I was able to take an incredible image of the moon.

Saturn

After observing the moon, I was able for the first time to create an image of Saturn. Truly incredible that we are able to see with a 300€ telescope planets that are millions of km away.

Milky Way - No need for a telescope!

Before I bought my first telescope, I was able to take this amazing image of the milky way. You really only need a camera, a tripod a bit of patience. This was done without tracking on a 30mm lense.

Progress over just a year

Knowing how to process images is as important as good quality equipment. Learning how to make the most of my camera has converted these small images of blobs into galaxies.

M13 - Hercules Cluster

One of my main objectives is to create a poster with all Messier objects. At the moment I'm starting with the most interesting Deep Space Objects, but the end result is going to be amazing!

Startrails

My main objective is to have fun and show people how the world works. Startrails like these are very easy to take. If you haven't tried to do one, give it a try! Look for an interesting subject, get a tripod, and just start taking photos!

My Home Telescope

Slowly but surely I have built a small telescope. One of the great aspects of astrophotography is the modularity: I can upgrade each piece individually as needed.

Enrique Galceran - Astrophysicist

I am a last year PhD Candidate, working on Star Formation History and metallicity evolution determination with the use of Neural Networks. I love to take pictures of the sky while listening to audiobooks. I enjoy programming and building small videogames.
